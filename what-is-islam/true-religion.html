The True Religion

Exploring the criteria for a true religion and why Islam fulfills those criteria — through logic, evidence, and the testimony of its scripture and history.

Throughout history, humans have sought answers to life's most fundamental questions: Where did we come from? What is the purpose of our existence? What happens after death? How should we live? These questions point to the human need for religion — a framework of meaning, morality, and connection to the Divine.

For a religion to be true, it must meet several criteria. First, it must be internally consistent — its teachings must not contradict themselves or reason. Second, it must offer a coherent account of God, creation, humanity, and ethics. Third, its scripture must be authentic and preserved. Fourth, its moral teachings must align with human nature and promote human flourishing.

Islam claims to be the original, universal religion of God — the same message delivered through all prophets from Adam to Muhammad. It invites critical examination of its claims. The Quran frequently challenges its readers to reflect, reason, and consider the evidence: 'Do they not think deeply?' (7:184), 'Will they not then reason?' (36:68).

The Quran's preservation is itself remarkable. Unlike other ancient scriptures that exist in multiple, often contradictory versions, the Quran has been preserved in its exact original form since its revelation 1,400 years ago, memorized by millions of people across every generation.

The rapid and enduring spread of Islam — despite the humble origins of its founder, the simplicity of its message, and the tremendous opposition it faced — is itself evidence of its divine origin. Within 100 years of its founding, Islam spread from Arabia to Spain in the west and China in the east, attracting diverse peoples through the power of its message.

Islam invites every sincere seeker to investigate its claims honestly. It does not demand blind faith but encourages an active, questioning engagement with its scripture and teachings. 'Read in the name of your Lord who created' (96:1) — the very first word revealed in the Quran is an invitation to engage with knowledge.
