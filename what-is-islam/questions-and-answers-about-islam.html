Questions and Answers About Islam

Answers to common questions about Islam — covering its beliefs, practices, misconceptions, and the lives of Muslims around the world.

Q: What does the word 'Islam' mean? A: The word 'Islam' is derived from the Arabic root 'salama', meaning peace, purity, submission, and obedience. In a religious context, it refers to complete submission to the will of God. A follower of Islam is called a Muslim, meaning 'one who submits to God'.

Q: Is Allah a different God from the God of Christians and Jews? A: No. The Arabic word 'Allah' simply means 'God' — the same God worshipped by Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. Arab Christians also use the word 'Allah' for God. Islam affirms that there is one God, the Creator of the universe, who sent guidance through all the prophets including Jesus and Moses.

Q: Do Muslims worship Muhammad? A: No. Muslims do not worship Muhammad (peace be upon him). He was a human being — the final prophet of God. Muslims honor and respect him deeply, but worship is reserved for God alone. When a Muslim hears his name, they say 'peace be upon him' as a sign of respect, not veneration.

Q: Why do Muslim women wear the hijab? A: Muslim women wear the hijab (headscarf) as an act of modesty and obedience to God's command. It is not a sign of oppression but of dignity and identity. Many Muslim women freely choose to wear it as an expression of their faith and their refusal to be defined by their physical appearance.

Q: Why do Muslims pray five times a day? A: Prayer (Salah) is the direct link between a Muslim and God. The five daily prayers serve as constant reminders of God's presence throughout the day, providing spiritual nourishment, moral grounding, and a pause from worldly concerns. They are also an expression of gratitude to God for His countless blessings.

Q: Is Islam a violent religion? A: No. Islam means peace, and its central teachings emphasize justice, mercy, and compassion. The Quran states: 'Whoever kills a person, it is as if he has killed the entire humanity, and whoever saves a person, it is as if he has saved the entire humanity.' (5:32). The vast majority of Muslims are peaceful people who condemn terrorism and violence.
