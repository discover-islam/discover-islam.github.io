Some Basic Islamic Beliefs

An overview of essential Islamic beliefs including monotheism, prophethood, the afterlife, and the Quran as the final revelation.

At the heart of Islam is Tawheed — the absolute monotheism, the belief in one God with no partners, equals, or intermediaries. This concept of God's Oneness distinguishes Islam from religions that associate partners with God. The Quran states: 'Say: He is Allah, the One. Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is born, nor is there to Him any equivalent.' (112:1-4)

Muslims believe that the Quran is the literal word of God, revealed to the Prophet Muhammad through the Angel Gabriel over 23 years. It is the final and complete revelation to humanity, preserved in its original Arabic text without any alteration since its revelation 1,400 years ago.

The concept of Prophethood in Islam holds that God sent messengers to every nation throughout history to guide humanity. These prophets were human beings chosen for their moral excellence. They were not divine but were protected from sinful behavior. The last prophet, Muhammad, was sent to all of humanity.

Muslims believe in the existence of the soul (ruh) within every person. At death, the soul departs the body and enters an intermediate realm called the Barzakh, awaiting the Day of Resurrection. This belief in the soul's continuity gives Islam its orientation toward eternal values over temporary worldly gains.

The concept of Qadr (divine decree) in Islam does not negate human free will. Muslims believe Allah has complete knowledge and that everything occurs with His permission, but that humans have been given the ability to choose between right and wrong, and are responsible for those choices.

Islamic beliefs emphasize that this worldly life is a test and preparation for the eternal life to come. The goal of a Muslim's life is not material accumulation but the acquisition of righteousness (taqwa) — consciousness of God — which alone secures success in the hereafter.
