Questions for Evolutionists

A series of thoughtful questions challenging the philosophical assumptions of evolutionary theory and pointing toward the necessity of a Creator.

'Evolution' in this context refers to the idea that natural, undirected processes are sufficient to account for the existence of all natural things. If so, consider the following questions.

Something from nothing? The Big Bang theory states that everything developed from a small dense cloud of subatomic particles and radiation. Where did this energy and matter come from? How reasonable is it to assume it came into being from nothing? We know from experience that explosions are destructive and lead to disorder — how could a Big Bang produce increasing information, order, and useful structures like stars, planets, and eventually people?

Physical laws an accident? The universe is governed by fundamental physical laws — electromagnetic forces, gravity, conservation of mass and energy. How reasonable is it to say these great controlling principles developed by accident? A computer program cannot function without existing hardware with an instruction set — how can the universe's precise physical laws be self-originating?

Order from disorder? The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that systems become more disordered over time without directed energy input. Evolutionists claim the opposite has occurred — that order increased over time without directed energy. Without a directing intelligence, raw energy cannot be converted into biological information.

The information in DNA — far more complex than any human-engineered code — had to originate somewhere. Information does not spontaneously arise from matter. We always observe information arising from minds. The stunning information content of living cells points unmistakably to an intelligent Source.

The Quran invites this reflection: 'We will show them Our signs on the horizons and within themselves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth.' (41:53). The natural world, examined honestly, bears witness to its Creator.
