The Basis of Muslim Belief for New Muslims

A clear and accessible introduction to the core beliefs of Islam for new Muslims and those exploring the faith.

For someone new to Islam, understanding the foundational beliefs is the first step. Islam's core beliefs are simple, rational, and profound. They are the Six Articles of Faith: belief in Allah, in His angels, in His revealed books, in His messengers, in the Last Day, and in divine decree.

Belief in Allah: God is One, with no partners, children, or equals. He is eternal — He was before anything existed and will remain after everything else ceases. He is the Creator, Sustainer, and Ruler of all creation. He knows everything, can do everything, and is present everywhere through His knowledge and power. He is both majestic and merciful, just and forgiving.

Belief in Angels: Angels are created from light and do not possess free will in the way humans do. They carry out God's commands perfectly. The Angel Gabriel brought divine revelation. Angels protect humans, record their deeds, and will be present on the Day of Judgment.

Belief in the Books: God sent scriptures to guide humanity — including the Torah (Moses), the Psalms (David), the Gospel (Jesus), and the Quran (Muhammad). Only the Quran has been preserved in its original form. The Quran confirms the original messages of all previous scriptures.

Belief in the Messengers: God sent messengers to every people throughout history — from Adam to Muhammad. They were human beings, not divine, but they possessed the highest moral character and were protected from major sins. Muslims respect and love all the prophets. Muhammad is the last and final prophet.

Belief in the Last Day and Divine Decree: Every soul will be resurrected and held accountable for its deeds. The righteous will be rewarded with Paradise; the wrongdoers will face justice. And behind all of this is Allah's divine decree — His complete foreknowledge and governance of all that occurs, while human beings retain real moral freedom and responsibility.
