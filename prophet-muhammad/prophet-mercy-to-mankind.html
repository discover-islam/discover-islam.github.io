Muhammad: Mercy to Mankind

An exploration of how the Prophet Muhammad embodied mercy in his dealings with his family, companions, opponents, and all of creation.

Allah describes the Prophet in the Quran: 'And We have sent you not but as a mercy for the worlds.' (21:107). This verse captures the comprehensive nature of the Prophet's mercy — it extended not just to Muslims but to all of humanity, and indeed to all living beings.

The Prophet's mercy with his family was remarkable. He said: 'The best of you are those who are best to their families, and I am the best to my family.' He helped with household chores, mended his own clothes, and treated his wives with affection and respect. He wept at the death of his son Ibrahim, saying: 'The eyes shed tears and the heart is sad, but we do not say anything except what pleases our Lord.'

His mercy extended to his enemies. When he was brutally rejected by the people of Ta'if and stoned until his feet bled, he refused the Angel Gabriel's offer to destroy them, saying: 'Perhaps their descendants will worship Allah.' When he conquered Mecca — the city that had persecuted him for years — he forgave his enemies and granted a general amnesty.

The Prophet showed extraordinary mercy to animals. He said: 'There is a reward for serving any living being.' He forbade the branding of animals on their faces, prohibited fighting animals for sport, and commanded kindness to animals during slaughter. He once rebuked a man who had taken baby birds from their nest, causing their mother distress.

His mercy toward non-Muslims set a high standard of religious tolerance. He said: 'He who wrongs a Jew or Christian will have me as his adversary on the Day of Judgment.' Under his governance, non-Muslims enjoyed full protection of life, property, and religious freedom.

The Prophet Muhammad is, in the words of one historian, 'the most influential single figure in human history.' His impact on humanity — in religion, civilization, law, ethics, and science — continues 1,400 years after his death, a testament to the enduring power of his mercy and his message.
